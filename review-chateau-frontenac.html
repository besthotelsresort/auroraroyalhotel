I have lost count of the hotels I have written up over twelve years, but I can count on one hand the ones that made me stop on the pavement and simply look up. The Château Frontenac is on that hand. Rising in copper-roofed turrets above the St. Lawrence, it is said to be the most photographed hotel in the world, and standing in the Place d'Armes at dusk, camera-less for once, I understood why. It does not look like a hotel. It looks like the reason a city exists.

Opened in 1893 and named for a French governor of New France, it is the grande dame of Old Québec — a UNESCO-listed quarter that unfolds from its doorstep in cobbled lanes and church spires. To stay here is to sleep inside the postcard, and after three nights I can tell you the interior mostly lives up to the silhouette.

Grandeur, and the reality of a heritage room

The public spaces are magnificent — soaring, panelled, gently theatrical — and the service carries the warmth that turns grandeur into hospitality. On my anniversary trip (I confess a personal weakness for this place) the staff sent up chocolates and a small bottle of prosecco without being asked. Guests report the same again and again: thoughtful people who remember the occasion. Breakfast is excellent, the Fairmont Gold lounge a genuine cut above, and a St. Lawrence view from your window is worth every effort to secure.

Now, the honest part. This is a 130-year-old building, and heritage has trade-offs. Some standard rooms are compact — one guest measured theirs at sixteen square metres — with small bathrooms and air-conditioning that hums the way old systems do. A motion-sensor minibar has caught more than one guest out with a surprise charge, so mind your elbows. And because the lobby is a bona fide attraction, it can fill with day-trippers; the trick is to retreat upstairs, where the hotel becomes yours again.

You are not merely booking a room here. You are borrowing a piece of Canadian history for the night — creaks, quirks and all.

Setting the standard for location

No hotel I know sits more perfectly inside its city. Step out and you are on the Dufferin Terrace, the boardwalk that runs along the cliff with the river glittering below; turn the other way and Old Québec's restaurants and boutiques are a two-minute stroll. For a traveller who wants to walk everywhere and return to something splendid, the Frontenac's position is close to unimprovable.

The spa and indoor pool are a lovely refuge after a day on the cobbles, and Bar 1608 pours one of the more atmospheric cocktails in the province. If I have a note, it is that a hotel charging a premium should not ask guests to jump through a Wi-Fi registration hoop — a small friction that a handful of guests, reasonably, resent.

The verdict

The Château Frontenac is not a flawless modern hotel, and if you require a vast bathroom and silent climate control you should know that going in. But you do not come here for those things. You come for the turrets and the terrace, the river light in the morning, the sense of staying somewhere that matters — and for service that treats your visit as an occasion. On those terms it is superb, and it remains my first recommendation in Québec City.
