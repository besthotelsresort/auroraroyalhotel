There is a particular kind of relief that comes from stepping off a red-eye flight and knowing your bed is a seven-minute train ride away. That is the promise River Rock makes before you even see it, and on the night I arrived — bleary, over-caffeinated, wheeling a case with one dying wheel — it kept that promise with a calm efficiency I did not expect from a building that also happens to house a 24-hour casino.

The Canada Line deposits you almost at the door. You walk in past a lobby Starbucks, and the first thing that strikes you is the water. River Rock sits right on the Fraser, and my river-view suite looked out over the marina at boats nodding in the dark. It is a genuinely handsome setting, and one that most airport hotels would kill for. My suite was large — properly large, the kind where your luggage does not become furniture — and blessedly quiet once the door closed on the corridor.

The stay: comfort with a few frayed edges

Let me be even-handed, because the guests who leave reviews here are. The bones of this place are excellent: the location is unbeatable for anyone flying in or out of YVR or boarding an Alaska cruise, the beds are comfortable, and the staff I dealt with — particularly at the concierge desk — were warm and quick. More than one recent guest has called their suite "one of the best hotels we've stayed at," and after my first night I understood the sentiment.

But I review with a checklist, and River Rock is a resort that is showing its age in places. The soft furnishings in my room had seen a lot of guests; a lamp base was chipped, and the television was resolutely not a smart TV in an era when travellers expect to cast their own shows. A recurring theme in guest feedback — and one I can corroborate — is that housekeeping is a little inconsistent. My room was clean, but a friend on the same floor found dust where there shouldn't have been. When a property positions itself as four-diamond, these are the details that decide whether the badge feels earned.

The location is the headline act here; the rooms are a solid, slightly weathered supporting cast.

The casino, the pool and the food court

The gaming floor is the beating heart of the resort — over a thousand slots, a wall of tables, a poker room that never sleeps and the River Rock Show Theatre pulling in genuine touring acts. If you want the energy of a casino floor, it is right there; if you don't, the hotel wing is far enough removed that you can ignore it entirely. That separation is River Rock's cleverest trick, and I slept without hearing a single jackpot bell.

The indoor pool with its waterslide is a hit with families, and the hot tub was blissfully warm after a long day. The dining is where opinions split. There are ten-plus outlets, but the food court in particular feels frozen in time, and a few guests grumble — fairly — that it hasn't evolved in years. I'd steer you toward the sit-down restaurants and the late-night noodle bar over the court.

A word on the bill

I would be doing you a disservice not to mention the most consistent complaint I found in recent reviews: confusion over charges and card holds. Several guests describe authorizations that tied up more credit than expected, sometimes at the worst possible moment before a cruise. I did not personally hit a problem, but I'd advise checking your pre-authorization at check-in and keeping an eye on your statement. Forewarned is forearmed.

The verdict

River Rock is a resort you book for what surrounds it as much as the room itself — the train at the door, the river out the window, the round-the-clock buzz for those who want it and the quiet for those who don't. It is not flawless: the rooms need a refresh and the billing needs a firmer hand. But for a first or last night in the Vancouver area, few places make the logistics this easy or the setting this pleasant. Go in with your eyes open on the details, and it delivers.
